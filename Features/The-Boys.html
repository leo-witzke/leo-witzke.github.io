The Boys Meta-Marketing Strategy

Analysis – Posted October 19, 2022

Image: Prime Video

The theater lights dimmed as the brash voiceover came in over generic action title cards. This was just another superhero movie trailer. The streets were destroyed in an obviously staged manner. All the clips seemed to be from one scene color graded puke yellow. What's this movie about beyond the voiceover’s buzzword-filled prattle on justice and sacrifice? The ending implied there was context to the trailer I was missing, but I’m usually up-to-date on movie news. What was that trailer for? And who is still making movies like that?

A couple weeks later, I decided to figure it out. The trailer was for Prime Video’s The Boys (2019-Current). Specifically, it was for Dawn of the Seven – a movie inside The Boys’ universe. Notably, the trailer made no reference to The Boys, the show it was advertising. Even the trailer’s YouTube comment section didn’t name the show; rather, it was overflown with praise for the fake movie. Why make a trailer like that?

The Boys is a satire on superheroes that imagines a company, Vought, has the power to create Supes (as they’re referred to on the show) by injecting normal humans with a secret formula, Compound V. Vought makes money off the superheroes' images through branded theme parks, burger chains, beverages, television, movies, and anything else they can put their Supes on. To make a trailer for a Vought movie (and make it terrible) is to give potential fans insight into the show's politics and give current fans an added layer of immersion. Comment sections enable fans to shape and enhance that immersive environment: co-creating more effective and engaging advertisements.

The Dawn of the Seven trailer is one of many blind advertisements made for Vought. Not restricted to movie trailers, Vought has accounts on all major SNS (Social Network Sites) that post canon content usually not included on the show. Mike Benson, former head of marketing at Amazon Studios, explains The Boys’ fake advertisements: “we love this idea of blind advertising that you're not quite sure if it's real or fake. The idea of not breaking the fourth wall so people may not realize what it is … Our whole objective is to get people curious and dive deeper. We know that from a customer journey standpoint, people will go out and they'll search” (Benson 2019). While these advertisements are meant to pull in potential fans, the comments sections on Vought’s SNS, specifically YouTube, are more reflective of an established audience seeking further engagement with The Boys and its fans.

Take The Boys’ parody of Kendal Jenner’s infamous Pepsi commercial. The fake commercial features A-Train (played by Jessie T. Usher), a member of Vought’s premiere superhero team, attempting to sell his branded A-Train’s Turbo Rush, a beverage comparable to Pepsi. A top comment on the YouTube video reads: “This is exactly why we all love A-Train, he's a guy with heart.” On the surface, this comment reads as a fan immersing themselves in The Boys’ universe. The commenter is embracing Vought’s brand to pretend the show is real and they’re part of it. However, fans of the show will recognize the double entendre at play. A-Train’s heart is deteriorating due to his abuse of Compound V; he’s losing his heart. If you’ve seen the show, you’ll find inside jokes in many comments. In effect, comments on Vought’s videos are simultaneously acknowledging the show to engage with the fan base and overlooking the show to engage with Vought’s brand.

A-Train’s Pepsi parody commercial posted to Vought’s YouTube channel.

Corporate messaging is boring, so “marketers should concentrate on capturing and sustaining attention via engagement. This paradigm shift makes it necessary for companies to engage their customers and co-create” (Yadav et al. 2016, 259–71). Prime Video, with the help of The Boys’ fans, has done this for The Boys on YouTube by making use of the comment section.

Co-creation is the collaboration between companies and customers to develop something new (Rashid et al. 2019, 761–84). SNS comments aren't commonly regarded as co-creation; take Kendal Jenner’s Pepsi commercial as a case study. Comments, on SNS and elsewhere, forced Pepsi to withdraw the commercial, but that wasn’t collaborative nor did it develop something new for Pepsi. In The Boys’ case, however, comments add fan meta-commentary to Vought’s videos by creating another layer of immersion and incorporating inside jokes; the comments collaborate by building off the advertisement’s premise that Vought is a real company. Prime Video has found a way to co-create in the comment section, and co-creation is making advertisements more engaging and effective to fans (Yadav et al. 2016, 259–71).

However, Prime Video’s meta-marketing strategy for The Boys hasn’t seen the same success on other SNS. Take the same A-Train Turbo Rush advertisement on Instagram and Twitter. The posts feature the same video and accompanying text, adjusted to the platform's format.

Some of the Instagram comments follow the same double entendre form found on Vought’s YouTube channel, but the majority mock or call attention to the parody (acknowledging Vought isn’t real). The Twitter replies are a mixed bag: some The Boys memes, a couple people’s thoughts on the show (engaging the fans but not the brand), and many others too sparse to classify. 

Overall, there is less engagement and co-creation on Twitter because it's the least socially interactive. Out of Twitter, Snapchat, Instagram, and Facebook, Twitter is used the least for social interaction (Alhabash and Ma 2017). Since fans ordinarily engage with Vought via videos (i.e. they engage through the show), YouTube’s emphasis on videos (compared to Instagram’s emphasis on photos and Twitter’s emphasis on text) explains why YouTube has the most engagement and co-creation. On Instagram, the video is cropped to a vertical aspect ratio, cutting off portions of the advertisement and detracting from immersion. This could explain why Instagram comments have the same tone as YouTube comments, but acknowledge Vought isn’t real.

Furthermore, by examining Vought content posted to non-Vought SNS accounts, we can see fans need Vought branding to immerse themselves. The Dawn of the Seven trailer was reposted to Rotten Tomatoes TV’s YouTube channel, but comments mainly focused on how funny or cool the trailer was. Even with the authority that comes with the Rotten Tomatoes’ brand as a hub for TV and movies, fans didn’t co-create with the trailer. Similar Vought-branded commercials posted to Prime Video’s YouTube channel generate a lot of positive comments from fans, but few references to Vought. Fans only co-create when posts come from Vought; they need an immersive environment.

Fake Soy Sauce commercial featuring The Deep (played by Chace Crawford) posted to Prime Video’s YouTube channel.

Fans interact with Vought content to immerse themselves in The Boys; the less Vought’s content connects to The Boys, the less co-creation in comment sections. Buster Beaver's (a pizza and games chain comparable to Chuck E. Cheese) is pivotal to Black Noir’s (played by Nathan Mitchell) season 3 arc. But when Prime Video posted a Buster Beaver's commercial to Vought’s YouTube channel, they didn’t reference Black Noir or Vought. Without the context of season 3, it’s unclear how this commercial connects to The Boys. While there is co-creation in the comment section, fans also used comments to reflect on Buster Beaver's and Black Noir’s season 3 fate. The commercial has a weak connection to The Boys, so fans aren’t immersed in it, and comments are used less for co-creation. Fans want to interact with The Boys, not Vought or any Vought subsidiary; on its own, Vought’s brand isn’t enough to garner co-creation from fans.

Vought’s SNS accounts immerse fans in The Boys by enabling them to co-create advertisements thematically tied to the show. Fostering co-creation is about creating the environment. Platforms that pull emphasis away from Vought’s videos see less engagement – making YouTube the ideal platform for Vought. If content doesn’t come from Vought, immersion is broken, and fans find other ways to engage. Posting to Vought’s YouTube channel doesn’t guarantee co-creation, the video has to have a strong connection to the show. In the gaps between seasons, co-creating advertising campaigns retains loyal fans (Yadav et al. 2016, 259–71) and offers a unique immersion into The Boys.
